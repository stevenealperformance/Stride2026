§ 03  —  Strategy

How to play this.

A proposal is the start of a negotiation, not the end. A few principles worth holding in mind.

  1. Anchor with questions, not numbers. Until we know whether Cavendish invested £50k or £500k, the equity conversation is half-informed. The discovery questions in §09 come before the final numbers.
  2. NDA before cap table. A simple mutual NDA lets both sides talk openly. It’s easier for founders to sign one in writing than to agree to be candid on a call.
  3. Offer structures, not concessions. If they push back on the headline terms, the right move is to offer two or three alternative shapes that land at roughly the same value — not to drop the number.
  4. Stay warm. A calibrated, curious tone outperforms firmness at this stage. We’re proposing a long partnership; the negotiation is the first working session.
  5. Protect the walk-away. If the deal isn’t right, being able to leave it graciously matters more than closing it. Templates in §12 handle that without burning the relationship.

§ 06  —  Full proposal

Scope & milestones.

I would own the coach-and-athlete product area end to end. Four workstreams, twelve months, four phases.

1. Coach–athlete relationship

  • Coach accounts, athlete rosters, invitation and on-boarding flows.
  • Permissions model: what athletes see, what coaches see, what stays private.
  • Multi-coach and coach hand-off scenarios (common in real clubs and federations).

2. Communication

  • In-app messaging tied to specific workouts, weeks, and training blocks — not a generic chat.
  • Workout-level feedback: athlete RPE, notes, symptoms; coach comments and adjustments.
  • Weekly and monthly structured check-ins, so communication stays high quality without becoming a time sink.
  • Notifications and nudges designed so neither side feels spammed.

3. Reporting

  • Automatically generated athlete reports: readiness, load, progression, adherence.
  • Coach dashboard showing a stable of athletes at a glance, right alerts surfaced first.
  • PDF / shareable reports for athletes, sponsors, parents, federations.
  • Integration of existing analytics (race analysis, fatigue, FTP estimation) into the reporting flow.

4. Coaching methodology in the platform

  • Structured intake questionnaire for new coach–athlete relationships.
  • Testing protocols (threshold, VO2, metabolic) surfacing into plans and reports.
  • An opinionated coach workflow based on proven methodology, so less-experienced coaches can deliver a higher standard of service through Stride than they could alone.
  • Optional “one-off coach review” product (publicly floated by Alex Barlow) — I can design and potentially staff the pilot.

12-month roadmap

Month 1
Discovery

Interviews with 10–15 working coaches; audit of existing Stride coach-side flows; written product brief and roadmap; agreed definition of “done” for Phase 1.

Months 2–4
Phase 1 — foundations

Coach account, athlete roster, invitation flow, core permissions, workout-level communication. Closed beta with a hand-picked group of coaches from my network.

Months 5–7
Phase 2 — reporting

Athlete reports, coach dashboard, shareable PDF. First public coach-tier release.

Months 8–10
Phase 3 — methodology

Intake, testing protocols, guided coach workflow, optional “coach review” pilot.

Months 11–12
Review & renew

Full review, coach NPS and retention data, decide on year-two roadmap and terms.

Explicitly out of scope unless we agree otherwise: core infrastructure, billing, AI model training, device integrations, and anything unrelated to the coach–athlete experience.

§ 08  —  Discovery questions

What I need from them first.

Before firming up numbers, a short call under NDA to cover the following. Some of these will change what a fair deal looks like.

Company stage & funding
  1. Funding to date — have you raised external capital, and if so at what round size and post-money valuation?
  2. Who is on the cap table today besides the two of you — angels, advisors, friends and family?
  3. Current runway and plan for the next raise, if any?
  4. Aiming to stay founder-controlled, or open to institutional VC?
Revenue & growth
  1. Roughly how many paying subscribers today, and what’s your monthly growth rate?
  2. Split between monthly and annual subscribers?
  3. Any early data on churn, LTV or CAC you’d share under NDA?
Team & roles
  1. Who is currently on the team full-time, part-time, and as contractors?
  2. Is anyone else actively working on the coach-athlete side?
  3. Who would I report to / collaborate with day to day?
Scope expectations
  1. Does my read of the coach-athlete scope (§06) match what you had in mind?
  2. How much of my time did you imagine — advisory, part-time, or near full-time?
  3. What does success look like for you at 6 and 12 months?
Compensation structure
  1. What equity ranges have you given existing advisors or team members?
  2. What vesting schedule is already in use for founder equity and anyone else’s?
  3. Share class preferences: ordinary, growth, or something else?
IP & exit
  1. Are there any non-standard IP, non-compete or non-solicit clauses I should know about?
  2. What’s the exit thesis — build to scale, build to sell, or long-term business?
  3. Good-leaver / bad-leaver treatment, and acceleration on change of control?
§ 09  —  Follow-up · Scenario A
They say yes in principle

The good case.

The trap here is losing momentum to vague enthusiasm. The move is to convert warmth into concrete next steps without over-pushing. Lock a call within 5–10 days, get the NDA signed before the call, and send the discovery questions in advance.

Subject: Great — let’s get a call in the diary
Hi Alex and Alex, Really pleased you’re up for taking this forward. A few practical things to get us moving: 1. Call. I could do [day/time 1], [day/time 2] or [day/time 3] — all UK time. 45 minutes should cover it. Let me know what works and I’ll send an invite. 2. NDA. I’ve attached a short mutual NDA so we can talk openly about funding, revenue and cap table on the call. If you’d rather use your own template, just send it over. 3. Prep. If you can come to the call with answers to even half of them, we’ll get through the real work much faster. No need to prepare anything formal; rough numbers are fine. Looking forward to it. Steve
§ 10  —  Follow-up · Scenario B
They push back on terms

The re-shape.

Most common pushback is “we love the idea but the equity ask is high” or “we can’t do a stipend that size right now.” That’s not a rejection — it’s an opening bid. Don’t drop your number in email. Ask what’s driving the constraint, then offer structures that land at roughly the same total value.

Subject: Re: Proposal — happy to work the shape
Hi Alex and Alex, Thanks for coming back on this — and for being straight with me rather than just saying yes and worrying about it later. Before I put a revised number on the page, it would help me to understand what’s driving the push-back. Totally fine to keep it high-level, but roughly: is it more about the cash side, the equity side, or the overall size of the commitment? Those are quite different problems and I’d rather solve the right one. To give you a sense of how flexible I can be, here are three alternative shapes I’d happily consider: • Cash-lighter, equity-heavier: £3,000/month stipend with equity raised toward 4%. Makes sense if cash is the tight constraint. • Cash-heavier, equity-lighter: £6,000/month stipend with equity reduced toward 2%. Makes sense if you’re protective of the cap table for a future raise. • Milestone-weighted: lower monthly stipend (£3,000) plus a one-off payment on each Phase milestone, plus 2.5% equity. Rewards delivery and keeps monthly burn low. All three get us to roughly the same value for me, just spread differently. Happy to shape a fourth if none of these land right. Shall we still do the call as planned? Much easier to work this through on a call than over email. Steve
Tactical: don’t volunteer a fourth, lower option in this email. Let them tell you which of the three is closest, then shape a final version. Offering four looks like negotiating against yourself.
§ 11  —  Follow-up · Scenario C
They go silent

The graceful close.

Silence is almost never about the proposal. Dowsett is in race season, Barlow is shipping, Roberts is running growth. The proposal is sitting in three full inboxes. Wait 7–10 days, send a short value-add nudge that gives them permission to say “not now.” If still silent after another 10–14 days, close the loop gracefully. After that, stop. Chasing past that point damages your leverage if they do come back.

Template 1 — the soft nudge (send 7–10 days after the original)

Subject: Re: Proposal — coach & athlete module for Stride
Hi Alex and Alex, Just floating the proposal back up in your inbox. No pressure at all — I know how busy the first half of the season is. One small thing that made me think of you: [drop in a specific, genuine observation here — a feature idea sparked by using the app, a coach you’ve spoken to who’d be interested, an article you read about a competitor, etc.] Happy to wait until you’ve got a clearer window. If the answer is “not now but keep in touch,” that’s a perfectly good answer too — just let me know so I can plan. Steve
The line “not now but keep in touch is a perfectly good answer too” is load-bearing. It removes the guilt that keeps busy people from replying, and dramatically lifts reply rates.

Template 2 — the final follow-up (send 10–14 days after the nudge)

Subject: Closing the loop
Hi Alex and Alex, I’m going to stop chasing on this for now — you’ve got plenty going on and I don’t want to be that guy. If the timing wasn’t right or the shape wasn’t quite what you were after, no hard feelings at all. I think what you’re building is genuinely interesting and I’ll be watching how it develops. If things shift later in the year and you’d like to pick this back up, you know where to find me. And if you ever want an honest coach’s perspective on a specific feature you’re shipping, my door’s open — no proposal attached. Good luck with the season. Steve
Three jobs at once: removes social pressure, signals professional self-respect, leaves a real door open. Founders often come back months later to emails exactly like this.
